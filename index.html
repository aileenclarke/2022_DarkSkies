But today, the Milky Way is hidden from 80% of people in North America.

99% of the continent lives under skies that experience some level of light pollution.

The culprit: nighttime lights that have proliferated since Edison invented the lightbulb.

Streetlights, car headlights and indoor lights are reflected skyward, causing a “sky glow” effect.
This constant light is wreaking havoc on human and wildlife physiology.

And it’s getting worse.

The advent of LEDs may have been a boon for energy efficiency,
but their blue-white color temperature has increased light pollution considerably.

Other trends have also illuminated previously dark places.
A boom in natural gas drilling has lit swaths of rural North Dakota and Texas.

Urban sprawl has filled former green space with lights.

The number of places that experience true dark skies is dwindling. The International Dark-Sky Association (IDA) works to protect these islands of darkness through their Dark Sky Places program. Since its founding in 2001, the program has recognizd over 130 places in North America for their night skies. The program has spurned policies that reduce light polution affecting skies from the nearly 2-square-mile Geauga Observatory Park on the outskirts of Cleveland to a 1,400-square-mile swath of central Idaho.

Image from the International Dark-Sky Association

Flagstaff, Arizona became the IDA’s first Dark Sky City in 2001. A city ordinance seeks to "assure that dark skies remain a resource to be enjoyed by the Flagstaff community and its visitors, and to provide safe and efficient outdoor lighting regulations that protect Flagstaff’s dark skies from careless and wasteful lighting practices. Dark starry nights, like natural landscapes, forests, clean water, wildlife, and clear unpolluted air, are valued in many ways by the residents of this community,"

Image from the International Dark-Sky Association

Public awareness about the value of darkness is growing. The number of Dark Sky preserves and communities has increased rapidly over the past decade.

Dark Sky parks, sanctuaries and reserves tend to be in remote locales far from most of the country's inhabitants. In 2019, the IDA began recognizing Urban Night Sky Places—spots in urban areas that are managed to maintain night skies in the midst of significant artificial light.
